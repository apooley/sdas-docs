## What the SDAS CRM Connector does

`sdas-crm-connector` syncs lead and vehicle data into CRMs such as Dynamics, HubSpot, Salesforce, and generic REST targets, with queuing and retry behavior to handle downstream issues.

<Callout kind="info">

The SDAS CRM Connector runs behind the scenes. It does not render form fields, buttons, or widgets by itself. It controls what happens to lead data after submission, not how the form looks or behaves in the browser.

</Callout>

Front-end teams still play an important role: you decide how visitors interact with forms and what expectations you set about follow-up, while the connector ensures those submissions are delivered into CRM systems.

## Front-end surface area

From a theme or page-builder perspective, the CRM Connector has no direct surface area on the public site.

<Callout kind="info">

The CRM Connector is admin and server-side only. It provides **no shortcodes**, **no blocks**, **no widgets**, and **no front-end JavaScript or CSS** for visitors. All of its logic runs in WordPress hooks and background queues after the site accepts a submission.

</Callout>

Use your existing form plugins, SDAS front-end components, or custom templates for anything visitors see. Treat the connector as an integration layer that receives events from those flows and pushes data into CRM targets.

## What triggers CRM sync

The connector listens for specific WordPress actions to decide when to queue CRM sync work. These actions map to concrete user-facing moments on the site, even though visitors never see the hooks directly.

### `sdas_lead_submitted`

This action fires when a lead is created and accepted by the site.

Typical front-end flows that eventually fire `sdas_lead_submitted`:

- A visitor submits a **contact or enquiry form** for a vehicle or department.
- A visitor books a **test drive**, **appointment**, or **trade-in** evaluation.
- A marketing form (for example, **newsletter opt-in**) creates a new lead record.

Once this action runs, the connector:

- Queues a job to send the lead into configured CRM destinations.
- Uses background processing (Action Scheduler) to retry if the CRM is temporarily unavailable.

From the visitor's point of view, this still looks like a normal successful form submission.

### `sdas_vehicle_description_generated`

This action fires when the system generates or updates a structured description for a vehicle.

On the front end, this usually corresponds to:

- A vehicle being **created or updated** in SDAS Inventory.
- A change that affects what visitors see on **vehicle detail pages** (for example, updated trim, pricing notes, or marketing copy).

When `sdas_vehicle_description_generated` runs, the connector:

- Queues a sync so CRM systems receive up-to-date vehicle metadata.
- Keeps CRM records aligned with what visitors see in listings and detail pages.

### `sdas_vehicle_sold`

This action fires when a vehicle is marked as sold in SDAS systems.

In user-facing terms, this often lines up with:

- A vehicle disappearing from **search results** or showing as **sold** on detail pages.
- A sales workflow in the back office that finalizes the deal.

When `sdas_vehicle_sold` runs, the connector:

- Queues an update so CRM records reflect the sale.
- Helps sales and marketing teams work from accurate inventory and deal status, without any extra front-end calls.

Admin users can also trigger manual or bulk resync actions, but those happen in `/wp-admin` and do not change front-end flows.

## How it interacts with other layers

The CRM Connector is one layer in the broader SDAS stack. Front-end work typically interacts with SDAS Forms Extension and Inventory, which in turn raise events the connector consumes.

### SDAS Forms Extension and lead creation

On the visitor side:

- SDAS Forms Extension (or your chosen form system) renders the **form UI**.
- The visitor fills out fields and submits, and the site validates and saves the lead.
- When the lead is successfully stored, SDAS Forms Extension fires `sdas_lead_submitted`.

At that moment, the CRM Connector:

- Listens for `sdas_lead_submitted` and **queues a background task** to sync the lead into configured CRMs.
- Does not block the HTTP response to the visitor while it waits for CRM responses.

Design your UI around the success of the **site-level submission**, not around direct confirmation from the CRM.

### Background queues, retries, and visitor messaging

Because the connector uses background queues and retries:

- CRM sync may complete **seconds or minutes after** the visitor submits.
- Retries can continue if the CRM is slow or temporarily offline.
- Operations teams can resync or inspect queues in the admin without involving front-end changes.

Front-end guidelines:

- Confirm that the **site received the submission** (for example, thank-you page, inline success state).
- Avoid phrasing that implies **instant CRM updates** (for example, "Your record is now live in Salesforce").
- If operations report delays or maintenance, adjust copy to set expectations about **response time**, not about CRM status.

For vehicle-related flows, treat `sdas_vehicle_description_generated` and `sdas_vehicle_sold` the same way: they are system events that keep CRM data aligned with what visitors see, but they do not change how you render pages or handle submissions.

## Where the connector is configured

Admins configure CRM destinations, credentials, and mapping behavior in the SDAS CRM Connector plugin settings at `/wp-admin` (under the SDAS plugins area).

Front-end developers and site builders typically do not change these settings directly, but you should know that:

- The connector expects certain contact and vehicle fields to be present in submissions.
- Admins can add or change CRM destinations without changing the site theme.
- Queuing and retry behavior is handled server-side, independent of the front-end.

Coordinate with your admins when you introduce new lead flows or change form structures so the connector continues to receive the fields it needs.

<Tab title="Visitor impact" icon="users">

Visitors interact with your forms and thank-you pages the same way they would on any site. The main difference with the SDAS CRM Connector is what happens to their data once they submit.

### What visitors see (and do not see)

Visitors:

- See **your form UI**, built with your form plugin, theme, or custom components.
- See **your validation messages**, loading states, and thank-you pages.
- Do not see CRM-specific details, queue states, or retry attempts.
- Do not get real-time confirmation from the CRM; confirmation comes from your site.

The connector runs after the form submission completes. From the browser's point of view, the submission is just a normal POST to your site.

### Setting the right expectations

Because the connector includes queuing and retry behavior, CRM delivery might not be instantaneous in every case. Design your front-end to:

- Confirm that the **form was received**, not that it was saved inside a CRM.
- Use phrases like **"We received your information"** or **"Our team will contact you soon"**, not **"Your data is now in our CRM"**.
- Avoid showing CRM-specific promises (for example, "check your CRM task list in 2 minutes") that depend on immediate downstream processing.

If CRM delivery is temporarily delayed, users should still feel confident that their lead was captured correctly by the site.

### Copy/paste: visitor confirmation copy

Use this ready-made copy to keep visitor messaging aligned with background CRM delivery. Adjust tone to match your brand, but keep the intent and promises the same.

<CodeGroup tabs="Thank-you page,Inline success,Internal-only note">

```text
Thank-you page body (generic)

Thank you for getting in touch.

We have received your details and shared them with our team. A member of our staff will review your enquiry and contact you as soon as possible.

If you contacted us about a specific vehicle, we will check its latest availability before we reply. If you submitted this outside of our normal opening hours, we will follow up on the next business day.
```

```text
Inline success message (short)

Thank you, we have received your details. Our team will be in touch soon.
```

```text
Internal-only ops note (staging / QA)

Internal note (not visible to visitors):

The site has accepted this submission and queued it for processing. Background jobs will attempt to deliver the lead to connected CRM systems shortly.

If you are testing in staging or QA, verify both:
- The form submission shows as successful on the site.
- The corresponding record appears in the CRM within the expected processing window.

This system uses background queues and retries, so CRM records may not update instantly even when the site confirms receipt.
```

<Tab title="Site-builder checklist" icon="code">

This checklist covers the coordination points between your front-end implementation and the SDAS CRM Connector. Work through it whenever you add or change lead forms.

### 1. Confirm admin configuration

Before making front-end changes, ask an admin:

- Which CRM destinations are active for this site.
- Which forms or lead flows are expected to feed the CRM.
- Which fields are required on their side to create or update records.

This gives you a target for what your forms need to collect, without exposing CRM-specific details to visitors.

### 2. Ensure forms collect required contact info

Design forms so they always capture enough information for meaningful CRM records. At minimum:

- Include **contact identifiers** such as name, email, and/or phone.
- Include **context fields** that matter for follow-up (for example, vehicle interest, department, or campaign).
- Match field purposes to what admins expect, even if the field names or IDs in your form system are different.

<Callout kind="tip">

If you change field labels, add new options, or remove fields, tell your admin team. They may need to update connector-side mappings to keep CRM sync accurate.

</Callout>

### 3. Do not promise instant CRM processing

Update your UI copy so it aligns with asynchronous delivery and possible retries:

- Use **generic confirmations**: "Thanks for reaching out. We will be in touch soon."
- Avoid strict timing: do not promise "within 5 minutes" unless you monitor that operationally.
- Keep any CRM mentions internal; visitors should not need to know which system you use.

### 4. Handle submission states clearly

Your form should:

- Show a **clear success state** when the server accepts the submission.
- Handle **client-side errors** (validation, network issues) with specific messages.
- Avoid showing low-level errors that imply CRM failures or connector internals.

If the site returns a success response, the connector will handle queuing and retries in the background. Do not block the UX waiting for CRM confirmation.

### 5. Coordinate when introducing new lead flows

When you add new lead flows, for example:

- New **vehicle enquiry** forms
- **Trade-in** or **test-drive** requests
- **Contact us** or **newsletter** opt-ins

Share with admins:

- Which URL or page the form lives on.
- The form system you used (for example, blocks, shortcodes, custom template).
- The fields you collect and how they relate to existing flows.

This helps ensure the connector routes and syncs the new leads correctly.

<Tab title="Operational notes" icon="settings">

Operational behavior affects how you talk about the system in documentation, training, and UI copy, even though visitors never see the internals.

### Queuing and retry behavior

The connector uses queuing and retry logic so that:

- Leads are not lost if a CRM is temporarily unavailable.
- Delivery can happen shortly after submission, not necessarily at the exact same second.
- Operations teams can investigate CRM connectivity without changing the theme.

From a front-end standpoint, treat CRM delivery as **eventually consistent**: the site accepts submissions, then backend processes work to sync them.

### What front-end teams should watch for

When you roll out changes:

- **Monitor form submissions** in your analytics or form system to confirm visitors complete flows as expected.
- **Ask admins** to confirm that corresponding CRM records appear as expected for a sample of leads.
- **Align messaging** with real operational behavior; if follow-up times change, update thank-you copy and automated responses.

### Handling edge cases in copy

For rare cases when operational teams detect delays, they may ask you to adjust UI or on-site messaging temporarily. Examples:

- Adding a banner or note that **response times may be longer than usual**.
- Adjusting expectations on pages that promise callbacks or appointments.
- Updating FAQs about how quickly the team responds to enquiries.

These changes stay entirely in your front-end and content; the connector continues managing delivery behind the scenes.
