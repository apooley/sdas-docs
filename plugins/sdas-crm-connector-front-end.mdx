## Key idea: server-side only, but admin-dependent

SDAS CRM Connector runs entirely in the background. It does not add shortcodes, blocks, widgets, or visible form elements to your site.

<Callout kind="info">

Treat SDAS CRM Connector as a **delivery engine**: once your site accepts a lead, the connector works behind the scenes to send that data into connected CRMs using queues and retries. Your job as an admin is to make sure forms collect the right information, thank-you messages set the right expectations, and test leads flow end-to-end.

</Callout>

You keep full control over how forms look, where they appear, and what visitors read when they submit. The connector depends on those flows to capture complete leads and push them to your CRM reliably.

---

## What visitors see on your site

Visitors interact only with your website forms and confirmation messages. They do not see CRM names, sync queues, or background jobs.

### Forms and fields

From a visitor's perspective:

- Forms appear in your **pages, posts, or templates**, using your usual tools (for example, SDAS Forms Extension, WPForms, block-based forms, or custom forms).
- Fields ask for **contact details** (name, email, phone) and **context** (for example, vehicle of interest, department, preferred date).
- Validation and error messages come from your **form plugin or theme**, not from the CRM.

Your forms should feel like any normal enquiry or booking form. The connector activates only after the site accepts a valid submission.

### Confirmation and follow-up messaging

After a successful submission, visitors should see clear confirmation that **your site** received their details, without mentioning technical steps like “sending to CRM.”

Good visitor experiences:

- A clear thank-you page or inline success message.
- A short explanation of **what happens next** and roughly **when** to expect a response.
- Optional follow-up details, such as opening hours or phone numbers.

Avoid promising anything that depends on instant CRM processing, because that part runs asynchronously in the background.

<Callout kind="info">

The connector uses **background queues and retries**, so CRM delivery does not always happen at the exact second of form submission. Design your copy around **"we received your details"** and **"our team will contact you"**, not around real-time CRM updates.

</Callout>

## What admins control in WordPress

As a WordPress admin or site builder, you control everything visitors see and how leads get created inside WordPress.

### 1. Which forms feed the CRM

Decide which forms on your site should create CRM records, for example:

- Vehicle enquiry or “book a test drive” forms.
- Trade-in valuation or finance request forms.
- General “contact us” or newsletter sign-up forms.

Coordinate with your operations or marketing team so they know **which lead flows exist** and where they appear on the site. They will configure the CRM Connector to listen for those leads and sync them onward.

### 2. Field completeness and quality

The connector can only deliver the data your forms collect. Make sure CRM-bound forms:

- Always include **contact identifiers**:
  - Full name, or at least first name and last name.
  - Email and/or phone number for follow-up.
- Include relevant **context fields**:
  - Vehicle of interest, department, enquiry type, preferred time.
  - Any information your team needs to respond effectively.
- Use field labels and options that **match how your team works** (even if the internal CRM field names are different).

If you change form fields later (add/remove options, rename labels, add new fields), tell the person who manages CRM Connector so they can keep mappings up to date.

### 3. Thank-you copy and automated replies

You manage all visitor-facing copy on:

- Thank-you pages.
- Inline success messages inside forms.
- Automated confirmation emails from your form system.

Use these messages to:

- Confirm that **the site received the enquiry**.
- Set realistic expectations for **response time**.
- Avoid naming the CRM or promising instant appearance in CRM dashboards.

#### Editable confirmation copy templates

Use or adapt these templates in your WordPress pages, form confirmations, or email notifications. Adjust tone to match your brand, but keep the intent similar.

<CodeGroup tabs="Thank-you page,Inline success,Email confirmation">

```text
Thank-you page body (generic)

Thank you for getting in touch.

We have received your details and shared them with our team. A member of our staff will review your enquiry and contact you as soon as possible.

If you contacted us about a specific vehicle, we will check its latest availability before we reply. If you submitted this outside of our normal opening hours, we will follow up on the next business day.
```

```text
Inline success message (short)

Thank you, we have received your details. Our team will be in touch soon.
```

```text
Email confirmation (optional)

Subject: We have received your enquiry

Thank you for contacting us.

We have received your details and shared them with our team. A member of our staff will review your enquiry and respond as soon as possible. If you contacted us about a specific vehicle, we will confirm its latest availability before we reply.

If you did not submit this enquiry, you can ignore this message. No further action is required.
```

</CodeGroup>

## How to coordinate with CRM configuration

You do not need to work with code or hooks, but you should understand how your content choices connect to CRM setup in the admin.

### Where CRM Connector is configured

Admins configure SDAS CRM Connector under `/wp-admin` in the SDAS plugins area. There they set:

- **Which CRMs** (for example, Dynamics, HubSpot, Salesforce, REST targets) receive leads and vehicle data.
- **Which events** from your site trigger CRM sync (lead submissions, vehicle updates, vehicle sold).
- **How fields map** from WordPress leads and vehicles into the CRM.

As a site builder, you rarely change these settings yourself, but your form design must align with them.

### What to share with the CRM admin

When you create or update forms that should feed the CRM, share with your CRM or operations owner:

- **Form locations**:
  - Page URLs where the forms live.
  - Which forms are new vs existing.
- **Form system**:
  - Whether you used SDAS Forms Extension, another plugin, or a custom form.
- **Fields and options**:
  - Which fields you collect (names, emails, phones, enquiry types).
  - Any new dropdown options or checkboxes they might want to track in the CRM.

This information helps them map your form fields correctly, so every new lead lands in the right place inside the CRM.

## Checklist: validate a new lead form end-to-end

Use this checklist whenever you add a new lead form or make significant changes to an existing one that should feed the CRM.

<Steps>

<Step title="Open the form on the live or staging site" icon="monitor">

- Navigate to the page where the form appears.
- Confirm the form **loads correctly** and shows all the fields you expect.
- Check that labels and help text make sense to a visitor.

</Step>

<Step title="Submit a realistic test lead" icon="play-circle">

- Fill in the form with **realistic test data**:
  - A test name (for example, "Test Lead SDAS").
  - A working email address you can check.
  - A phone number and any required context, like vehicle details.
- Submit the form and confirm:
  - You see the **expected success message** or thank-you page.
  - No obvious error messages appear after submission.

Success signal: the site clearly confirms that it received the enquiry.

</Step>

<Step title="Confirm the lead exists in WordPress" icon="database">

- In `/wp-admin`, open the area where leads are stored (for example, SDAS Forms Extension leads, form plugin entries, or a custom leads list).
- Locate your **test lead** and check:
  - All key fields (name, email, phone, vehicle, enquiry type) are populated.
  - The timestamp matches when you submitted the test.

Success signal: the test lead appears exactly once with complete data.

</Step>

<Step title="Wait for the expected CRM sync window" icon="clock">

- Ask your CRM or operations owner how long CRM sync normally takes (for example, within a few minutes).
- Wait at least that long before checking the CRM.
- Remember that queues and retries may mean the lead appears slightly later if the CRM is slow.

Success signal: enough time has passed for normal processing to complete.

</Step>

<Step title="Confirm the record in your CRM" icon="check-circle">

- With your CRM owner, search in the CRM for:
  - The test name you used (for example, "Test Lead SDAS").
  - Or the email address from the test submission.
- Verify that:
  - A new contact or lead record exists.
  - Key fields (contact details, vehicle interest, enquiry type) match the WordPress lead.
  - The lead appears in the **expected pipeline, list, or queue** for your team.

Success signal: the test lead appears correctly in the CRM with usable data.

</Step>

<Step title="Adjust copy or fields if needed" icon="edit">

- If the CRM owner sees missing or confusing data:
  - Update form fields or labels on the WordPress side.
  - Ask them to adjust mappings on the CRM Connector side.
- If visitors need clearer expectations:
  - Tweak thank-you messages or email confirmations based on real response times.
- Repeat a quick test submission if you make significant changes.

Success signal: everyone is confident that real visitors will be captured in WordPress and appear in the CRM with the right information.

</Step>

</Steps>

## Advanced (developers)

Most admins do not need to work with hooks or code. This section is only for developers who want to understand when the CRM Connector runs, so they can extend or troubleshoot integrations.

<Callout kind="info">

If you are not writing code, you can ignore this section. The important part for admins is that **once the site accepts a lead**, the connector listens for events and queues CRM sync in the background.

</Callout>

### Key WordPress actions

Developers should know that SDAS CRM Connector listens to these internal actions:

- `sdas_lead_submitted` — fired when the site creates and accepts a lead record.
- `sdas_vehicle_description_generated` — fired when SDAS Inventory updates a vehicle description that visitors see.
- `sdas_vehicle_sold` — fired when a vehicle is marked sold in SDAS systems.

These actions trigger background jobs that send lead and vehicle data to configured CRM destinations using Action Scheduler or a similar queue.

### Example: listening for a submitted lead

Developers can attach their own logic to the same events without affecting the connector:

<CodeGroup tabs="PHP">

```php
<?php
// Example: run custom code when a lead is submitted.
// The CRM Connector also listens to this action in the background.
//
// Action signature:
// do_action( 'sdas_lead_submitted', $lead_id, $lead_data );

add_action( 'sdas_lead_submitted', function ( $lead_id, $lead_data ) {
    // Prepare a short, safe summary for logging.
    $name  = isset( $lead_data['name'] ) ? $lead_data['name'] : '';
    $email = isset( $lead_data['email'] ) ? $lead_data['email'] : '';

    $parts = array( 'Lead submitted: ID ' . $lead_id );

    if ( $name !== '' ) {
        $parts[] = 'name ' . $name;
    }

    if ( $email !== '' ) {
        $parts[] = 'email ' . $email;
    }

    error_log( implode( ' | ', $parts ) );
}, 10, 2 );
```

</CodeGroup>
